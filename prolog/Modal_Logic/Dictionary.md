# Preface {#preface .unnumbered}

This dictionary provides concise entries on key philosophical concepts relevant to understanding mathematics as an emancipatory discipline. Entries are organized alphabetically and draw primarily from German Idealism (particularly Hegel), Critical Theory (Habermas), and cognitive science (Lakoff and Núñez). Cross-references appear in *italics*.

# A

## Algorithmic Elaboration

*Algorithmic elaboration* designates the process through which implicit norms governing a practice become explicit through formal, mechanizable procedures [@Brandom:2019aa]. This represents a particular mode of pragmatic expressive bootstrapping where the inferential commitments already operative in material reasoning become articulated through algorithmic specification. When we formalize arithmetic operations, we do not create something entirely new; rather, we make explicit the normative structure already guiding our informal calculations. The algorithm codifies what practitioners already understood implicitly through their competent performance. This concept challenges the formalist assumption that algorithms constitute the essence of mathematical activity. Instead, algorithmic elaboration reveals how formal procedures emerge from and remain answerable to pre-formal practices. The development from intuitive counting to formal arithmetic exemplifies this process: the Peano axioms do not ground arithmetic but rather elaborate the normative commitments already operative in competent arithmetical practice. See also *Material Inference*, *Pragmatic Expressive Bootstrapping*.

## Alienation

*Alienation* (*Entfremdung*) designates the condition wherein individuals experience estrangement from the products of their activity, from others, and ultimately from themselves. Marx articulates alienation as emerging from specific social relations rather than from an inevitable human condition. Under capitalist production, workers confront the products of their labor as alien powers, as commodities belonging to others. This extends beyond mere economic relations: alienation fractures the recognition structures through which self-consciousness achieves reality. When labor becomes commodity, the normative space of mutual recognition collapses into strategic calculation. Habermas develops this analysis through the concept of systematically distorted communication, where instrumentalized relations masquerade as genuine understanding [@habermas1992individuation]. Alienation thus represents not simply an unfortunate side effect of economic arrangements but a fundamental disruption of the intersubjective conditions for human flourishing. The struggle against alienation requires transforming the material conditions that prevent reciprocal recognition. See also *Recognition*, *Strategic Action*, *Communicative Action*.

## Anaphora / Anaphoric Term

*Anaphora* denotes linguistic expressions that refer back to previously established content without repeating the original reference [@brandom1994making]. Anaphoric terms (such as "it," "that," "the same") enable discourse to maintain referential continuity while avoiding the instability of indexical expressions. When I say "This is a cat" and then "It is sleeping," the pronoun "it" recollects the content established by the initial indexical "this" without requiring renewed ostension. Brandom emphasizes that anaphora provides the mechanism through which fleeting indexical reference becomes stable enough for inference [@brandom1994making]. Without anaphoric recollection, each assertion would remain isolated, unable to serve as a premise for subsequent reasoning. This has profound implications for understanding numerical reference: if numerals function as anaphoric terms, they do not name pre-existing objects but rather recollect the iterative activity of counting itself. The numeral "three" would then mark a position in the anaphoric chain generated through repeated applications of successor. This interpretation challenges Platonist ontology while explaining how mathematical discourse achieves stability despite lacking direct ostensive grounding. See also *Indexicals*, *Null Representation*.

## Apperception

*Apperception* designates the unity of self-consciousness that accompanies all acts of thought, constituting the transcendental condition for the possibility of objective knowledge [@Kant1781]. Kant argues that the "I think" must be capable of accompanying all representations; otherwise, representations would not belong to a unified consciousness and could not constitute knowledge [@Kant1781]. This is not empirical self-awareness but the logical form of self-relation internal to thought itself. Apperception is self-consciousness in its most fundamental sense: the mind's recognition of its own activity as its own. Negarestani emphasizes that apperception only functions within a "deprivatized semantic space in which recognitive cognitive agents are individuated" [@Negarestani2018 271]. This social dimension challenges readings that treat apperception as a private, inner capacity. The unity of apperception cannot be achieved by an isolated subject; it requires the normative space instituted through mutual recognition. Apperception thus designates both the logical form of self-consciousness and the socially constituted space within which this form achieves reality. See also *Self-Consciousness*, *Recognition*, *Geist*.

## Absolute Negativity

*Absolute negativity* constitutes the dialectical foundation of self-movement in Hegel's philosophy [@Hegel:2014aa]. Unlike determinate negation, which negates a specific content, absolute negativity is self-referential negation that negates its own negating activity. This double negation generates the dynamic structure of the *Concept* (*Begriff*), enabling thought to develop through its own internal contradictions rather than through external determination. Absolute negativity is the "innermost source" of activity because it makes the Concept self-moving and self-determining [@Hegel:2014aa]. See also *Concept*, *Dialectic*, *Vernunft*.

# B

## Basic Metaphor of Infinity

The **Basic Metaphor of Infinity (BMI)** maps the inferential structure of completed iterative processes onto processes that continue indefinitely, metaphorically adding a "final resultant state" [@Lakoff2000]. This conceptual metaphor is essential for understanding actual infinity in mathematics, including infinite sets, limits, transfinite numbers, and infinitesimals. Without the BMI, statements about infinite entities lack determinate meaning. For instance, projective geometry's "line" and its properties depend on this metaphor for their intelligibility. The BMI demonstrates how abstract mathematical concepts emerge from embodied cognitive mechanisms rather than from pure logical necessity. See also *Grounding Metaphors*, *Linking Metaphors*.

## Becoming

*Becoming* (*Werden*) designates the dialectical unity of being and nothing, the most fundamental category of Hegel's logic [@hegel_georg_2010]. Neither pure being nor pure nothing can stand alone as determinate thought; each immediately passes over into the other. Becoming is not their mere alternation but their truth---the movement in which they exist as moments of a single process [@hegel_georg_2010]. This is not temporal succession but the logical structure of determinateness itself: anything that is must be determinate, which requires negation, yet this negation is not external but internal to what the thing is. The concept of becoming captures how Hegel understands reality as fundamentally processual rather than static. Determinateness emerges through development; things are what they become. This applies equally to consciousness, which exists only as the movement of self-differentiation and return to itself. Hyppolite emphasizes that becoming represents "the living dialectic of the particular and the universal" [@Hyppolite1974]. For mathematics, this suggests that numbers should not be understood as fixed objects but as positions within a generative process---as nodes in the movement of iterative elaboration. See also *Dialectic*, *Sublation*, *Determinate Negation*.

# C

## Critical Mathematics

*Critical mathematics* designates an approach to mathematical education and practice that interrogates the social conditions under which mathematical knowledge is produced, transmitted, and deployed [@Gutstein:2006aa; @skovsmose_critical_2023; @Skovsmose:2014aa]. This orientation refuses to treat mathematics as politically neutral technique, instead attending to how mathematical practices can either reinforce or challenge structures of domination. Drawing on critical pedagogy, critical mathematics asks whose interests are served by particular framings of mathematical problems and by specific modes of mathematical reasoning [@Gutstein:2006aa]. The goal is not simply to democratize access to existing mathematical knowledge but to transform mathematics itself into a practice oriented toward emancipation. This requires examining how mathematics functions within systems of power and developing pedagogies that enable learners to use mathematics as a tool for understanding and transforming their social reality. Critical mathematics thus engages with both epistemological questions (what counts as mathematical knowledge?) and political questions (how does mathematics function in society?). It connects to broader traditions of critical theory by treating mathematical activity as a form of human practice embedded in concrete social relations rather than as access to abstract, timeless truths. See also *Alienation*, *Communicative Action*, *Power*.

## CUSP you

The *CUSP you* represents a distinctive mode of address emerging within dialogue that invokes the authority of an internalized community---the generalized other---to regulate self-presentation and constrain authentic expression [@Carspecken1999]. This "you" differs from the intimate, I-thou relation of genuine recognition; instead, it speaks from the standpoint of social norms, articulating what "one" should do or think. When someone says "you really shouldn't feel that way," they invoke this normative authority. The CUSP you mediates between the spontaneous I and the social "me," functioning as the voice through which the generalized other makes its demands felt in immediate experience. Mead's analysis of the self provides the theoretical foundation: the self develops through taking the attitude of others, ultimately internalizing the organized attitudes of the social group as the generalized other [@mead1934social]. The CUSP you is the grammatical trace of this internalization, marking moments where communal norms constrain individual expression. This concept proves crucial for understanding how power operates not only through external coercion but through the internalization of disciplinary norms. The CUSP you can function ideologically, masking particular interests behind appeals to universal standards. Yet it can also mediate genuine normative claims, expressing the legitimate authority of communal practices. The task is distinguishing between these uses. See also *Generalized Other*, *{I} vs. "me"*, *Recognition*, *Validity Claims*.

## Communicative Action

*Communicative action* characterizes social interaction where participants coordinate through mutual understanding rather than through strategic calculation or force [@Habermas1984; @habermas1992individuation]. Unlike *instrumental action* or *strategic action*, communicative action achieves coordination through the rationally motivating force of intersubjective recognition of validity claims. Participants adopt a performative attitude, seeking agreement about situation definitions by raising and redeeming claims to truth, rightness, and sincerity. Language functions not as a means of influence but as the medium through which understanding itself is achieved [@Habermas1984]. The bonding force of reaching understanding creates the normative foundation for social reproduction. Communicative action presupposes that speakers relate simultaneously to the objective, social, and subjective worlds [@Habermas1984]. See also *Instrumental Action*, *Strategic Action*, *Validity Claims*, *Intersubjectivity*.

## Concept (Begriff)

The *Concept* represents Hegel's understanding of thought's self-conscious structure [@Rodl:2019aa]. It is not a static mental representation but the dynamic unity of thinking that comprehends itself in its objectivity. The Concept is inherently self-determining, containing both universality and particularity within itself through the movement of *absolute negativity*. Rödl emphasizes that the objectivity of the Concept is the realization of self-consciousness [@Rodl:2018aa]. The Concept thinks itself, making thought both subject and object simultaneously. This self-reference constitutes the "constructive kernel" of *Geist* [@Negarestani2018]. The Concept is the foundation of rationality because it embodies the unity of self-consciousness and objectivity. See also *Absolute Negativity*, *Self-Consciousness*, *Objectivity*, *Geist*.

## Cultural Power

*Cultural power* designates control over the distribution and currency of cultural themes, limiting the roles and identity claims available to group members [@Carspecken1995aa 131--132]. Unlike *interactive power*, which operates through direct relations between actors, cultural power penetrates identity formation itself. It constrains the repertoire of subject positions individuals can occupy, shaping not merely behavior but the very constitution of selfhood. This power operates through symbolic resources, determining which identity claims achieve recognition and which remain unintelligible or devalued [@Carspecken1999 65]. Cultural power thus represents a deep form of domination because it precedes and conditions the possibility of resistance. See also *Interactive Power*, *Recognition*, *Intersubjectivity*.

## Caritas (Agape)

*A form of general, unconditional love for all beings.* Within Carspecken's framework, it is understood not merely as a psychological emotion but as an ontological principle with direct relevance to the ethical practice of ethnography.

The concept draws from the Greek agape, a central tenet in Christian theology signifying selfless, unconditional love, and its Latin equivalent, caritas.

Caritas is positioned as a necessary ethical response to the inherent problems of ethnographic representation, which he terms the Violence of Representation and Ontological Misrecognition.1 In the act of writing about another person, the researcher risks objectifying them, reducing their infinite subjecthood to a finite character in a narrative. Caritas is the ethical and ontological stance that actively resists this objectification. It is a commitment to recognizing and honoring the subject-nature of the Other, an act of love that precedes and informs the research process itself.

This concept is the ethical telos, or ultimate purpose, of his critical methodology. It is intrinsically linked to Ontological Forgiveness, which addresses the unavoidable harm of representation, and Recognition, which is the intersubjective actualization of this loving stance.

[@HenzeCarspecken2018]

## Critique

*A process of inquiry into the \"limits of knowledge.* \" For Carspecken, critique is not simply negative criticism or fault-finding; it is a rigorous, reflective investigation into the enabling and constraining conditions that make a particular form of knowledge possible. By revealing these foundational boundaries, critique opens a legitimate space for other ways of knowing.1

The concept is fundamentally Kantian. Carspecken explicitly states that Kant's Critique of Pure Reason \"supplies a core sense to critique as this term has been understood by critical theorists\".1

He expands Kant's project beyond the critique of pure reason to a critique of the entire \"empiricist framework\" and its ideological manifestation, Scientism.1 Critique becomes the primary philosophical method for revealing the Transcendental-like Conditions that make physical science possible but also limit its domain of inquiry. This act of delineating science's own limits serves to defend the legitimacy and necessity of the human and social sciences. The method by which critique is performed is Reflection.

This concept is foundational to Carspecken's entire project. It provides the philosophical justification for his inquiry into Limits of Knowledge and is the purpose of Reflection. It links his epistemological arguments to his political opposition to Scientism.

[@carspecken_limits_2016]

# D

## Determinate Negation

*Determinate negation* designates the dialectical process through which the negation of a specific content generates determinate results rather than abstract emptiness [@hegel1977phenomenology]. When consciousness confronts the inadequacy of a particular shape of knowledge, this negation is not mere skeptical rejection but produces a new, more determinate shape. The new standpoint emerges as the result of what preceded it, carrying forward the truth of the negated position while transcending its limitations [@hegel1977phenomenology]. Hegel argues that this distinguishes philosophical dialectic from abstract skepticism: "The skepticism that ends up with the bare abstraction of nothingness or emptiness cannot get any further from there, but must wait to see whether something new comes along and what it is, in order to throw it too into the same empty abyss" [@hegel1977phenomenology §79]. Determinate negation, conversely, "is a new form which has thereby arisen, and in the negation the transition is made through which the progress through the complete series of forms comes about of itself" [@hegel1977phenomenology §79]. This process structures the entire *Phenomenology*: each stage's collapse generates its successor through determinate negation. Brandom emphasizes that determinate negation embodies reason's capacity to learn from error by treating inadequate concepts not as arbitrary failures but as revealing determinate limitations requiring specific corrections [@Brandom:2019aa]. For mathematics, this suggests that mathematical development proceeds not through arbitrary invention but through systematic responses to internal inadequacies in existing conceptual frameworks. See also *Dialectic*, *Sublation*, *Becoming*.

## Dialectic

The dialectical method characterizes how thought develops through internal contradiction rather than through external determination. Hegel articulates dialectic as the "negative" moment where fixed determinations of *Verstand* dissolve into their opposites, revealing their one-sidedness [@hegel_georg_2010 para. 81]. However, dialectic is simultaneously positive, generating more concrete and comprehensive determinations through the negation of negation. The dialectical movement is not merely logical but constitutes the actual process of reality's self-development. *Vernunft* operates dialectically, comprehending contradictions as moments of a developing whole rather than as logical impossibilities [@Brandom:2019aa]. The dialectic of sense-certainty exemplifies this structure: immediate knowledge claims dissolve when articulated, revealing mediation and universality as essential [@hegel1977phenomenology]. See also *Verstand*, *Vernunft*, *Sense-Certainty*, *Absolute Negativity*.

## Deconstruction / Metaphysics of Presence / Trace

*This cluster of terms, drawn from Jacques Derrida, forms the basis of Carspecken's critique of traditional epistemology.*

Metaphysics of Presence: The foundational but, according to Derrida, mistaken assumption in Western philosophy that knowledge and truth can be grounded in a moment of pure, immediate, and unmediated access to a phenomenon, an object, or the self. It is the belief in a \"certain ground for knowing\" based on something being fully \"present\" to consciousness.1

Deconstruction: A strategy of analysis that reveals a philosophical or literary text's unacknowledged dependence on the unstable \"metaphysics of presence.\" Deconstruction shows how a text's own logic inevitably undermines its claims to certainty by revealing the internal contradictions that arise from this dependence.1

Trace: The (non)concept Derrida uses to describe the structure of experience once the illusion of pure presence is dismantled. Experience is never a pure presence but is always already a \"trace\" of a prior moment that was itself never fully present. This creates a structure of \"differing from\" and \"deferring to\" a perpetually absent origin. As Carspecken explains, \"The presence-of-the-present is derived from repetition and not the reverse\".1

These concepts originate in Jacques Derrida's critique of Edmund Husserl's phenomenology, a point Carspecken demonstrates a deep understanding of in his own analyses.1

Carspecken performs a critical appropriation of Derrida. He accepts the deconstruction of presence as a devastating and conclusive argument against all epistemologies based on a model of perception, including positivism and naive realism. However, where some might follow this critique to a \"ludic\" or nihilistic postmodernism, Carspecken uses it as a catalyst to clear the ground for a new foundation.1 The problem of presence demonstrates that any methodology based on an observer \"mirroring\" reality is flawed. Even many anti-positivist approaches, like constructivism, remain vulnerable because they still rely on modified perceptual metaphors such as \"lenses\" or \"windows\".1 This leads to the conclusion that a robust critical methodology requires a completely different starting point---an Originary Scene not based on perception at all. Carspecken finds this new scene in the communicative action of pragmatism. In this framework, the problem of certainty is reformulated not as achieving an impossible \"presence\" but as achieving mutual understanding and Recognition through dialogue, a process governed by its own rational standards (Validity Claims). He also reinterprets the \"longing for presence\" not as a mere philosophical error but as an existential \"desire for recognition,\" which can be productively, if never fully, addressed through communicative action.1

These Derridean concepts serve as the critical foil for Carspecken's entire epistemology. Their acceptance necessitates his rejection of Picture-thinking and his turn toward Praxis, Intersubjectivity, and a dialogical theory of truth.

[@Carspecken1999]

Dialectic

*A mode of thought that understands concepts and reality through the dynamic interplay of opposition, contradiction, and negation.* In Carspecken's specific usage, it is the process that reveals the nature of the Subject (\"I\"), which enters representational frameworks \"as the negative\"---that which is not an object but is the necessary condition for the experience of any object.1

The concept is rooted in Hegelian philosophy. Carspecken notes that \"dialectical philosophies like Hegel's are based on this insight\" of the \"I\" as the negative.1

Carspecken employs dialectics primarily as an epistemological and phenomenological tool rather than a historical-materialist one (as in orthodox Marxism). It serves to describe the self-contradictory attempt of Picture-thinking to represent its own conditions of possibility. The dialectic reveals the Limits of Knowledge by demonstrating that any attempt to objectify the subject or experience itself results in a logical impasse, thereby proving their non-objective nature.

Dialectic is the logical engine that drives his critique of objectivism and is intrinsically linked to his concepts of The \"I\"/Subject, The Infinite, and Reflection.

[@Carspecken2009aa]

# E

## Embodied Mathematics

Mathematics emerges from embodied cognitive mechanisms rather than from disembodied reason or Platonic forms [@Lakoff2000]. Mathematical concepts arise through *grounding metaphors* that map basic sensorimotor experiences (such as object collection, motion, and construction) onto abstract domains. For instance, arithmetic emerges from the embodied experience of grouping objects, and calculus develops from metaphors of motion and change. This account challenges the notion that mathematical truth is independent of human cognition. Instead, mathematical meaning is constituted through conceptual metaphors that structure our understanding. The universality of mathematics reflects shared embodied experiences across human cultures, not access to transcendent entities. This understanding has implications for mathematics education, suggesting that effective pedagogy must engage embodied cognitive processes rather than treating mathematics as pure symbol manipulation. See also *Grounding Metaphors*, *Basic Metaphor of Infinity*.

## Embodied Modal Logic

*Embodied modal logic* designates a framework for understanding modality (necessity, possibility, actuality) through proprioceptive and somatic experience rather than through abstract logical axioms. This approach grounds modal distinctions in the lived experience of bodily awareness practiced through contemplative exercises. When practitioners attend to the arising and dissolving of bodily sensations, they encounter the rhythm of possibility emerging into actuality and subsiding back into potentiality. This experiential grounding challenges the formalist assumption that modal logic must be axiomatically specified independently of phenomenological content. Instead, modal categories emerge from reflection on the temporal structure of awareness itself. The distinction between necessity and contingency, for instance, can be understood through the contrast between involuntary bodily processes (heartbeat, breath) and voluntary movements. Possibility manifests in the range of movements available to the body; actuality in the particular movement realized; necessity in the constraints structuring possible movement. This embodied approach to modality connects to Aristotelian potentiality-actuality dynamics while refusing the hypostatization of modal categories into separate metaphysical realms. It suggests that formal modal logic should be understood as making explicit the normative structure already implicit in embodied modal experience. See also *The Exercise*, *Embodied Mathematics*.

## The Exercise

*The Exercise* designates a specific guided meditation practice using proprioceptive awareness to engage the nature of self-certainty and the temporal rhythm of thought. Practitioners are guided to attend systematically to bodily sensations---feet, legs, hands, arms---observing how attention moves and how thought arises and dissolves. This practice provides experiential grounding for theoretical concepts including the Sound of Time and Embodied Modal Logic. The Exercise operates through sublation: practitioners let go of awareness of individual body parts to feel the whole, enacting the dialectical movement from particular to universal. This mirrors Hegel's analysis of sense-certainty, where the attempt to grasp the particular "This" reveals its universal structure. Through sustained practice, participants encounter the pre-reflective background that enables conscious thought---what the framework terms the Null Representation. The Exercise thus functions as the phenomenological foundation for the entire critical autoethnographic project. It refuses the separation of theory from practice by making bodily experience the ground from which philosophical categories emerge. This methodological choice challenges academic philosophy's tendency toward pure conceptual analysis, insisting that concepts must remain answerable to lived experience. The Exercise embodies praxis-grounding in its most literal form: philosophical insight emerging from disciplined attention to the body's own self-awareness. See also *Sound of Time*, *Null Representation*, *Embodied Modal Logic*.

## Existential Needs

*Existential needs* characterize fundamental human requirements that cannot be satisfied through empirical means alone [@Carspecken1999]. These needs concern identity, recognition, and the constitution of selfhood. The need for *recognition* is existential because it addresses "limits to knowledge precisely where knowledge is a feature of ontology" [@carspecken_limits_2016]. The desire to *be* involves self-knowing that cannot ultimately satisfy this desire. Recognition remains an "unreachable limit case" because privileged access to subjective experience makes total certainty about others' recognition impossible [@Carspecken1999]. Human being is thus a "communicatively non-satisfiable process of self-knowing" [@carspecken_limits_2016]. The identity claim seeks self-certainty of the infinite "I" by claiming a finite "me," producing inherent contradiction. This contradiction ontologically grounds the recognition need [@Carspecken1999]. Existential needs thus reveal anthropological structures that transcend particular cultural formations. See also *Recognition*, *Intersubjectivity*, *Self-Consciousness*.

## Expressive Action / Praxis

Expressive Action: A theory of action where the meaning of an act is not fully formed in the mind prior to execution but is clarified and comes into being through the process of expression itself. An act begins with a holistic, embodied \"impetus\" which becomes differentiated and more explicit as the act unfolds.1

Praxis: A concept defining human life as a fundamentally expressive, self-producing process. It refers to the essential human need to objectify oneself in the world through creative and meaningful activity, a process that simultaneously forms and transforms both the world and the self.1

This framework is drawn from the \"expressivist\" tradition of German Idealism (Johann Gottfried Herder) and the early philosophical writings of Karl Marx.1 Carspecken explicitly cites Charles Taylor's scholarship on this tradition.1

Praxis and Expressive Action form a cornerstone of Carspecken's social ontology, providing a dynamic, action-oriented alternative to the static, perception-based models he critiques. This framework grounds his analysis of embodied meaning and the role of the Feeling-Body. He argues that even the act of understanding another person is a form of praxis, as it involves generating \"impeti for new acts of our own\" in response.1

This is the positive philosophical content that fills the epistemological space cleared by his Deconstruction of Presence. It serves as the foundation for his concepts of Embodied Meaning and the Feeling-Body and is existentially motivated by the Desire for Recognition.

[@Carspecken1999]

# F

## Formal Reduction Metaphor

The *Formal Reduction Metaphor* provides the conceptual foundation for formalist mathematics by projecting set-theoretical structure onto any discretized mathematical domain [@Lakoff2000]. This metaphor prescribes conceptualizing mathematical subject matter in terms of sets and symbolizing it uniformly to mesh with formal logic. Mathematical elements map onto sets, relations onto ordered $n$-tuples, and functions onto constrained sets of ordered pairs. The metaphor has two interpretations: a cognitive interpretation where mathematicians blend subject matter with set-and-symbol structure, and a foundational interpretation where the metaphor is taken literally, reducing all mathematics ontologically to set theory and logic. The latter interpretation, central to formalism, claims that mathematical entities simply *are* sets and that mathematical truth is tautological. However, this metaphor presupposes other metaphors, particularly the *Spaces Are Sets of Points* metaphor, which discretizes continuous geometric intuition. The Formal Reduction Metaphor thus reveals how formalism depends on conceptual metaphors despite claiming to eliminate them. See also *Basic Metaphor of Infinity*, *Grounding Metaphors*.

## Feeling-Body / I-Feeling Mode

Feeling-Body: A phenomenological concept referring to the pre-conceptual, holistic, and embodied source of all meaningful action. It is the site of the \"impetus to act,\" which manifests in awareness as proprioceptive configurations, pre-kinesthetics, and pre-imagery.1

I-Feeling Mode: The singular, unifying aspect of the Feeling-Body's experience. Carspecken describes it phenomenologically as the \"fading sensation of a former fusion of awareness and impetus\".1 It is the most primordial, non-objective sense of self, which is \"going-gone as soon as we reflect upon it\".1

This is a sophisticated synthesis of Maurice Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology of the \"body-subject,\" Eastern spiritual philosophies (specifically the concept of mahatattva or the \"I-feeling\" in yogic thought), and expressivist theory.1

These concepts provide the deep phenomenological grounding for his theory of Expressive Action. The Feeling-Body is his attempt to describe the Originary Scene of experience before it is differentiated into the categories of subject and object. The \"I-Feeling Mode\" serves as the experiential counterpart to the philosophical concept of The \"I\"/Subject. He explicitly links it to Derrida's Trace, suggesting that the immediate, felt experience of the \"I-feeling-as-fading-away\" is phenomenologically prior to the more abstract concept of the trace.1

This is the experiential basis for Embodied Meaning and Praxis. The I-Feeling Mode is the phenomenological manifestation of The \"I\"/Subject.

[@Carspecken1999]

# G

## Geist (Spirit)

*Geist* designates the realization of reason as self-conscious and objective [@Hyppolite1974]. Hegel articulates Geist as the unity of self-consciousness and objectivity, where reason "knows itself to be conscious of itself qua its world and of the world qua itself" [@Hyppolite1974 325]. Geist is not individual consciousness but a normative social space constituted through reciprocal *recognition*. As Negarestani emphasizes, Geist embodies the "constructive spiral between intelligence and intelligibility," expressing the logic of self-reference [@Negarestani2018 25]. This logic applies specifically to sapient consciousness. Geist is essentially historical and developmental, emerging through collective practices rather than existing as a metaphysical substance. The objectivity Geist achieves is not correspondence to independent reality but the self-transparency of thought comprehending its own rational structure. Geist thus reconciles the modern insight into the attitude-dependence of norms with their objective authority [@Brandom:2019aa]. Language constitutes the medium of Geist because linguistic practices institutionalize the norms governing rational activity. See also *Recognition*, *Self-Consciousness*, *Concept*, *Vernunft*.

## Generalized Other

The *generalized other* represents the internalized attitude and collective expectations of one's social group or community [@mead1934social]. George Herbert Mead articulates this concept to explain how individuals develop social selves through taking the attitude not just of particular others but of the organized community as a whole. The generalized other embodies the "we" that shapes the social self (the "me") by providing the norms and standards against which actions are judged [@mead1934social]. This differs from the attitude of particular others: when playing baseball, the child must understand not only each teammate's perspective but the rules and structure of the game itself---the generalized attitude of the entire team. Development of the generalized other marks the transition from play (taking individual roles) to game-playing (coordinating multiple perspectives through shared rules). The generalized other makes self-consciousness possible in its full sense because it provides the standpoint from which the self can judge itself. This concept connects directly to the CUSP you, which invokes the authority of this internalized community. The generalized other can function both normatively (expressing legitimate communal standards) and ideologically (masking particular interests as universal norms). Habermas's concept of the ideal speech situation can be understood as articulating conditions under which the generalized other would express genuine communicative rationality rather than systematically distorted communication. See also *{I} vs. "me"*, *CUSP you*, *Recognition*, *Intersubjectivity*.

## Grounding Metaphors

*Grounding metaphors* map basic embodied experiences onto abstract mathematical concepts, constituting the cognitive foundation for arithmetic [@Lakoff2000]. Four primary grounding metaphors structure our understanding of numbers: (1) *Arithmetic as Object Collection* grounds numbers in experiences of gathering physical objects; (2) *Arithmetic as Object Construction* conceptualizes numbers as constructed wholes built from units; (3) *Arithmetic as Motion Along a Path* maps numerical operations onto spatial movement; (4) *The Measuring Stick Metaphor* understands numbers as lengths. These metaphors are "grounding" because they link abstract numerical concepts to sensorimotor experience rather than to other mathematical concepts. Different grounding metaphors highlight different aspects of numerical structure. For instance, the Object Collection metaphor naturally supports additive thinking, while the Motion metaphor facilitates understanding of sequence and succession. All grounding metaphors are valid simultaneously, creating a rich conceptual blend that constitutes mathematical meaning. This demonstrates that mathematical concepts are not discovered in Platonic heaven but constructed through embodied cognition. See also *Embodied Mathematics*, *Linking Metaphors*, *Basic Metaphor of Infinity*.

# H--I

## {I} vs. "me"

The distinction between the *{I}* and the *"me"* articulates the fundamental tension within selfhood between spontaneity and social recognition [@mead1934social]. The {I} represents the spontaneous, creative, unrepresentable source of action---the aspect of self that cannot be fully objectified or captured in any description. It is the moment of agency, the initiating impulse that exceeds social determination. The "me," conversely, is the social self---the self as objectified, categorized, and recognized by others according to social norms. When I act, the {I} initiates the action; when I reflect on that action, I grasp it as the "me" [@mead1934social]. This distinction proves foundational for understanding the dynamics of recognition and misrecognition. The {I} seeks recognition but cannot be adequately recognized because it is infinite and unrepresentable. What gets recognized is always the "me"---a finite, socially constituted identity. This generates the primary existential needs: the need to be recognized as "good" (conforming to social norms, developing an adequate "me") and the need to be recognized as "infinite" (expressing the uncontainable {I}). These needs are inherently contradictory yet both essential to human development. The tension between {I} and "me" drives the dialectical movement of self-development and structures the possibility of authentic versus inauthentic existence. When the "me" ossifies into a rigid identity that suppresses the {I}'s spontaneity, alienation results. Genuine recognition would acknowledge both dimensions---the social "me" and the infinite {I}---yet this remains an unreachable limit case. See also *Existential Needs*, *Recognition*, *Generalized Other*, *CUSP you*.

## Incompatibility Semantics

*Incompatibility semantics* provides a framework for understanding meaning through exclusion rather than reference [@brandom1994making; @Brandom2008]. Robert Brandom develops this approach as an alternative to representationalist semantics: the meaning of a concept is determined not by what it refers to but by the set of concepts it is incompatible with. For instance, "square" is partially defined by its incompatibility with "circular," "triangular," and "hexagonal." This approach proves particularly powerful for normative discourse because it explains how we can make meaningful assertions about abstract entities (like obligations or mathematical objects) without presupposing their independent existence. Incompatibility semantics grounds inferential relations: if two claims are incompatible, asserting one commits one to denying the other. This structures the space of reasons as a network of commitments and incompatibilities rather than as a mapping between representations and represented objects. For mathematics, incompatibility semantics enables a novel account of correctness: arithmetic error is not failure to match pre-existing mathematical facts but systematic violation of inferential incompatibilities. When someone claims $2 + 2 = 5$, they demonstrate incompetence in navigating the network of arithmetical incompatibilities. This approach allows errors to be incorporated into mathematical systems as meaningful elements rather than dismissed as mere falsehoods. Mathematical objects need not be independently existing entities; they can be understood as nodes in the inferential structure defined by incompatibility relations. See also *Material Inference*, *Pragmatic Expressive Bootstrapping*.

## Indexicals

*Indexicals* constitute linguistic expressions whose referent depends on the context of utterance [@brandom1994making]. Paradigmatic indexicals include "I," "here," "now," "this," and "that." Their meaning is fundamentally unstable: "now" refers to different moments each time it is used; "this" picks out whatever the speaker indicates in the present context. Because indexicals cannot create stable meaning independently, they require supplementation through anaphoric terms that can recollect their content and make it available for subsequent reasoning [@brandom1994making]. When I say "This is a cat" followed by "It is sleeping," the pronoun "it" anaphorically recollects the content established by "this," stabilizing the reference. Without such anaphoric mechanisms, each indexical use would remain isolated, unable to participate in inference. This has profound implications for mathematical reference: if numerals were purely indexical, pointing each time to abstract objects, mathematical reasoning would require continuous ostensive access to Platonic entities. However, if numerals function anaphorically---recollecting the iterative process of counting rather than directly referring to abstract objects---mathematical discourse can achieve stability without presupposing a Platonic realm. The numeral "three" would mark a position in the anaphoric chain generated through repeated successor operations rather than naming an independently existing number. This interpretation preserves mathematical objectivity while avoiding problematic metaphysical commitments. See also *Anaphora / Anaphoric Term*, *Null Representation*.

## Instrumental Action

*Instrumental action* characterizes purposive activity aimed at technical control of objective conditions [@Habermas1984; @Carspecken1995aa]. Unlike *communicative action*, instrumental action coordinates through causal efficacy rather than through reaching understanding. The actor selects means effective for bringing about desired states in the objective world. Success is measured by whether the intended state appears, not by whether understanding is achieved [@Habermas1984]. Instrumental action presupposes only the objective world of existing states of affairs, whereas communicative action presupposes simultaneous relation to objective, social, and subjective worlds. The rationality governing instrumental action is cognitive-instrumental rationality, focused on effectiveness and efficiency [@Habermas1984]. When language appears in instrumental action, it functions as a physical means of intervention rather than as a medium of understanding. The actor treats language strategically, using it to influence others toward predetermined goals [@habermas1992individuation]. Instrumental action can be embedded within broader communicative contexts, but its internal logic remains success-oriented rather than understanding-oriented. See also *Strategic Action*, *Communicative Action*.

## Interactive Power

*Interactive power* designates power relations where actors are differentiated by who has authority to define situations [@Carspecken1995aa 129]. Four forms characterize interactive power: *normative power* operates through shared norms and status positions (e.g., teacher-student relations); *coercive power* employs threatened sanctions; *contractual power* derives from explicit agreements; *charismatic power* wins authority through personality [@Carspecken1995aa 46, 130--131]. Unlike *cultural power*, which operates through symbolic resources and identity formation, interactive power functions in direct relations between actors. Interactive power can be legitimate or illegitimate depending on whether subordinates accept the validity of the authority relation. Habermas emphasizes that power distorts interactions oriented toward understanding when it operates without legitimation, preventing the "force of the better reason" from determining consensus [@Carspecken1999 46]. In communicative action, power must be neutralized or equalized to allow genuine understanding. Interactive power thus marks the boundary between communicative and strategic orientations to social coordination. See also *Cultural Power*, *Strategic Action*, *Communicative Action*.

## Intersubjectivity

*Intersubjectivity* designates shared understanding, mutual expectations, and reciprocal recognition among subjects [@Carspecken1999]. It is not a collection of individual subjectivities but a dynamic relational structure central to meaning constitution. Intersubjectivity is generated primarily through *position-taking*, where individuals implicitly or explicitly adopt others' perspectives, understanding acts from first-, second-, and third-person positions simultaneously [@Carspecken1999; @Carspecken1995aa]. This structure is essentially normative: intersubjective validity claims presuppose shared norms governing assertion, action, and expression. Language functions as the medium of intersubjectivity because understanding speech acts requires grasping the conditions under which their *validity claims* would hold [@Carspecken1999]. Habermas emphasizes that intersubjectivity constitutes a normative social space irreducible to individual consciousness or objective states of affairs. *Geist* is essentially intersubjective, realized through reciprocal recognition in linguistic practices [@Negarestani2018]. Intersubjectivity thus provides the transcendental condition for both knowledge and selfhood. See also *Position-Taking*, *Recognition*, *Geist*, *Validity Claims*.

# J--K

## Judgment (Urteil)

For Hegel, *judgment* constitutes the connection of self-subsistent totalities: subject and predicate [@Hegel:2014aa 553]. The German *Urteil* signifies "primordial division" of original unity, reflecting judgment's internal structure. Judgment is the self-diremption of the *Concept*, appearing as the separation of subject and predicate while implicitly containing their unity. The *Concept* constitutes judgment's essential ground. In judgment, the singular subject is raised to universality while the universal predicate descends into existence through the singular [@Hegel:2014aa 554]. Hegel distinguishes forms of judgment: the *judgment of existence* connects immediate abstract singular with immediate abstract universal; the *judgment of reflection* sublates qualitative character, making determinations reflect in each other; the *judgment of necessity* presents subject matter in objective universality, measuring reality against the "ought" embodied in predicates like "good," "true," or "right" [@Hegel:2014aa 578]. Rödl emphasizes that judging is inherently self-conscious: "judging that things are so \[is\] thinking it right so to judge" [@Rodl:2018aa 39]. Consciousness of validity is internal to judgment itself, not added through secondary reflection. See also *Concept*, *Self-Consciousness*, *Objectivity*.

## Knowledge Constitutive Interests

*Knowledge constitutive interests* designate species-wide orientations that determine knowledge's fundamental nature and purpose across domains [@Habermas:1971aa; @Carspecken1995aa]. Habermas identifies three such interests: the *technical interest* guides natural sciences, seeking prediction and control of objective processes through nomological knowledge; the *practical interest* orients interpretive sciences toward mutual understanding and the clarification of meaning in tradition and communication; the *emancipatory interest* drives critical sciences to identify and overcome ideological distortions that constrain human freedom [@Habermas:1971aa]. These interests are "constitutive" because they establish the transcendental conditions for knowledge in each domain, determining what counts as valid methodology, evidence, and truth. They are "knowledge" interests because they concern cognition specifically, and "interests" because they reflect fundamental human needs. Knowledge constitutive interests operate at the species level, reflecting anthropological constants in human life-activity. They relate to *validity claims* by providing the macro-level framework within which micro-level assertions gain meaning. The emancipatory interest is particularly significant for critical theory because it grounds the normative criterion for ideological critique. See also *Validity Claims*, *Communicative Action*.

# L--N

## Linking Metaphors

*Linking metaphors* connect one mathematical domain to another, extending concepts beyond their original grounding [@Lakoff2000]. Unlike *grounding metaphors*, which map embodied experience onto abstract concepts, linking metaphors operate entirely within mathematics. Examples include: the *Numbers as Points on a Line* metaphor, which projects arithmetic onto geometry; the *Natural Numbers Are Sets* metaphor, which enables arithmetic's reduction to set theory by conceptualizing zero as the empty set and each successor as the set containing its predecessor; *Cantor's Metaphor*, which redefines "same size" for infinite sets through one-to-one correspondence, diverging from everyday understanding. Linking metaphors are essential for mathematical sophistication because they enable systematization and unification of disparate domains. However, they can obscure the metaphorical nature of mathematical concepts by making mappings appear natural or necessary. The *Formal Reduction Metaphor* is a particularly significant linking metaphor, projecting set-theoretical structure onto all discretized mathematics. Linking metaphors thus reveal mathematics as a layered system of conceptual mappings rather than as a discovery of pre-existing abstract entities. See also *Grounding Metaphors*, *Formal Reduction Metaphor*, *Basic Metaphor of Infinity*.

## Material Inference

*Material inference* designates inference whose validity depends on the conceptual content of its non-logical terms rather than on formal logical structure alone [@brandom1994making; @Brandom2000]. Robert Brandom articulates this concept to challenge the formalist assumption that all valid inference must be reconstructible as formally valid. The inference "It is raining, therefore the streets will be wet" is valid because of what "raining" and "wet streets" mean, not because of its logical form (which would be simply $p \to q$, an invalid form). Material inferences are the primitive units of reasoning; formal logic emerges as an attempt to make explicit the patterns already operative in material inferential practice [@brandom1994making]. This reverses the traditional priority: instead of material inference being valid insofar as it instantiates formal validity, formal patterns gain their authority by codifying materially good inferences. For mathematics, this implies that arithmetical competence is fundamentally practical mastery of material inferential transitions (knowing that adding 2 to 3 yields 5) rather than grasping formal axioms. The Peano axioms, on this view, do not ground arithmetic but rather elaborate the norms implicit in competent arithmetical practice. Material inference thus provides the foundation for an "informal" approach to mathematics, one that refuses to reduce mathematical reasoning to symbol manipulation according to formal rules. See also *Incompatibility Semantics*, *Pragmatic Expressive Bootstrapping*, *Algorithmic Elaboration*.

## Null Representation ($\emptyset$)

The *Null Representation* ($\emptyset$) designates the use of the empty set symbol to represent the unrepresentable enabling conditions of thought itself. This is not the mathematical empty set but a philosophical symbol pointing toward what must be presupposed for representation to be possible yet cannot itself be represented. It stands for the transcendental "I think," the silent Voice that makes speech possible, or the "groundless ground" of consciousness. The function of $\emptyset$ is paradoxical: it is a representation that marks the limits of representation, a symbol that points toward what exceeds symbolization. This connects to Agamben's concept of the Voice---the negative, unsayable ground presupposed by meaningful language [@agamben_language_2006]. In the framework developed here, $\emptyset$ serves as the foundational element from which numerals are derived. If numerals function anaphorically, recollecting iterative processes, then $\emptyset$ represents the origin of this anaphoric chain---the unrepresentable beginning that enables all subsequent representation. This interpretation challenges both Platonism (which posits numbers as independently existing objects) and strict formalism (which treats zero as simply the first axiomatically given element). Instead, $\emptyset$ embodies the recognition that mathematical objectivity emerges from the self-conscious activity of thought reflecting on its own enabling conditions. The Null Representation thus functions as the philosophical symbol for what Kant called apperception, Hegel called absolute negativity, and what phenomenology calls the pre-reflective ground of consciousness. See also *The Voice*, *Anaphora / Anaphoric Term*, *Apperception*.

## Numerals

*Numerals* are linguistic expressions that refer to numbers, arising from language's self-conscious reflection on its own normative structure [@Brandom:2019aa; @Negarestani2018]. Brandom argues that numerals emerge when linguistic practice becomes explicitly aware of its commitments to repeatability and generality. Following Frege, Brandom emphasizes that numbers are inherently tied to concepts: to assert that there are two apples is to apply the concept *apple* with the cardinality operator *two*. This understanding grounds arithmetic in the normative structure of conceptual content rather than in empirical objects or Platonic entities. Numerals thus exemplify how *Geist* generates objective structures through self-reference. The development of numeral systems demonstrates the historical and social character of mathematical concepts. Different cultures develop different numeral systems, yet these systems share structural features reflecting universal aspects of embodied cognition and social practice. The capacity to use numerals presupposes mastery of *anaphora*, allowing linguistic expressions to refer back to previous utterances. This demonstrates that numerical cognition is fundamentally linguistic and normative rather than perceptual or pre-linguistic. See also *Geist*, *Embodied Mathematics*, *Grounding Metaphors*.

# O--P

## Objectivity

*Objectivity* characterizes thought whose validity depends solely on what is judged rather than on contingent characteristics of the judging subject [@Rodl:2018aa]. Rödl argues that objectivity and *self-consciousness* are identical: thought is objective precisely *because* it is self-conscious. Thinking "from the inside" involves apprehending oneself as thinking the judgment to be valid [@Rodl:2018aa]. This internal awareness frees judgment from dependence on the subject's "given character"---the empirical, contingent features knowable only from third-person position. Objectivity thus resides in self-consciousness itself [@Rodl:2018aa 11]. This contradicts the traditional opposition between objectivity and first-person position. Traditionally, achieving validity requires "breaking free from the first person," while self-consciousness places first person in every thought [@Rodl:2018aa 8]. Rödl demonstrates this opposition is "merely apparent" [@Rodl:2018aa 11]. Knowledge of object is simultaneously self-knowledge because thought, in its objectivity, thinks itself. Crucially, this self-consciousness is not empty: thought itself originates all content [@Rodl:2019aa]. Objectivity is therefore not correspondence to independent reality but thought's self-transparency and rational determinacy. See also *Self-Consciousness*, *Concept*, *Vernunft*.

## Position-Taking

*Position-taking* designates the process through which individuals adopt others' perspectives, constituting *intersubjectivity* [@Carspecken1999; @Carspecken1995aa]. This involves understanding acts from first-, second-, and third-person positions simultaneously. Position-taking is not optional perspective-shifting but the structural condition for meaning itself. To understand an utterance requires grasping how it would be experienced from the speaker's position, how it addresses the hearer, and how it appears from an observational standpoint. Position-taking generates normative expectations because adopting another's position means recognizing the validity claims implicit in their expressions [@Carspecken1999]. This structure explains how language achieves intersubjective coordination: shared understanding presupposes participants' capacity to occupy multiple positions simultaneously. Position-taking is fundamentally tied to *recognition* because acknowledging another as a subject means treating their position as a genuine source of claims rather than merely as an observable phenomenon. The capacity for position-taking distinguishes genuinely social interaction from merely behavioral coordination. See also *Intersubjectivity*, *Recognition*, *Validity Claims*.

## Power

*Power* designates capacity to influence or control, with profound implications for knowledge and identity [@Carspecken2018]. Foucault conceptualizes power as primordial and anonymous, existing prior to truth claims and subjectivity itself [@Carspecken1999 27--28]. Power circulates through "netlike organization," exercised through techniques and tactics of domination rather than possessed [@Flew1979]. The knowledge-power nexus is central: power produces knowledge, and knowledge constitutes power relations [@habermas1992individuation]. Habermas, conversely, emphasizes power's external relation to truth, defining power as that which distorts interactions oriented toward understanding [@Carspecken1999 46]. However, Habermas implicitly acknowledges a generative sense of power tied to agency: all human acts are powerful because they "make a difference" and issue from agents who could act otherwise [@Carspecken1999 49]. Power is thus tied conceptually to action itself. Communicative acts express power in relation to goals like being understood or recognized, intertwining "will to truth" and "will to power" with identity claims [@Carspecken1999 57, 67]. See also *Interactive Power*, *Cultural Power*, *Communicative Action*.

## Pragmatic Expressive Bootstrapping

*Pragmatic expressive bootstrapping* designates the dialectical process through which a system generates more expressively powerful vocabulary by making explicit the norms already implicit in existing practices [@brandom1994making; @Brandom2000]. This concept, developed by Robert Brandom, articulates how conceptual development occurs not through introducing wholly new content but through articulating what was already operative yet unarticulated. The paradigmatic example is the introduction of logical vocabulary: the practice of making material inferences (such as "It is raining, so the streets will be wet") already commits practitioners to certain patterns of reasoning. The introduction of conditional "if\...then" syntax makes these inferential commitments explicit, enabling practitioners to reason *about* their inferences rather than merely *making* them [@brandom1994making]. This is "bootstrapping" because the new vocabulary emerges from reflection on existing practice rather than from external sources; it is "expressive" because it increases the system's capacity to articulate its own norms; it is "pragmatic" because it begins with practice rather than theory. Hegel's logic can be understood as a sustained exercise in pragmatic expressive bootstrapping: each stage makes explicit what was implicit in the previous stage, generating increasingly adequate conceptual frameworks. For mathematics, this concept illuminates how mathematical development proceeds: the transition from natural numbers to integers, for instance, makes explicit the structure of inverse operations already operative in natural number arithmetic. The development is not arbitrary invention but systematic elaboration of implicit commitments. See also *Material Inference*, *Incompatibility Semantics*, *Algorithmic Elaboration*.

# R

## Recognition

*Recognition* (*Anerkennung*) constitutes the fundamental structure through which self-consciousness achieves reality [@hegel1977phenomenology]. Hegel establishes that self-consciousness "exists only in being acknowledged" [@hegel1977phenomenology §178]. Recognition is reciprocal: each self-consciousness achieves certainty of itself only through recognition by another self-consciousness. The famous master-slave dialectic demonstrates that asymmetric recognition fails because the master's recognition comes from one who is not recognized as free, rendering it worthless [@hegel1977phenomenology]. Genuine recognition requires mutual acknowledgment of autonomous subjects. Brandom emphasizes that recognition is normative: to recognize another is to attribute authority and responsibility, treating them as subject to and source of commitments [@Brandom:2019aa]. Recognition thus institutes the normative social space within which reason operates. Carspecken articulates recognition as an *existential need* that can never be fully satisfied because privileged access to subjective experience prevents total certainty about others' recognition [@Carspecken1999]. Recognition is the "unreachable limit case" grounding the desire to be [@carspecken_limits_2016]. This non-satisfiability drives historical development of recognition structures. See also *Existential Needs*, *Geist*, *Self-Consciousness*, *Intersubjectivity*.

## Reconstructive Analysis

*The core analytical process in Carspecken's critical methodology.* It is the systematic work of making explicit the tacit cultural knowledge, social rules, power relations, and Validity Claims that structure a given social interaction. In essence, it \"reconstructs\" the implicit Pragmatic Horizon of a meaningful act for critical examination.1

The method draws on several traditions, including ethnomethodology's attend to members' methods, hermeneutics' attend to interpretation, and Jürgen Habermas's project of \"rational reconstruction.\"

Reconstructive analysis constitutes Stage Two of his five-stage research process and is central to the entire methodology.1 It involves a detailed, often line-by-line analysis of primary records (such as interview transcripts and ethnographic field notes) to first articulate the plausible Meaning Fields and then to identify the underlying Validity Claims (objective, subjective, and normative-evaluative) that participants are implicitly making, negotiating, and responding to.

This is the practical application of his abstract theoretical framework. It is the method that uses the concepts of the Pragmatic Horizon, Meaning Fields, and Validity Claims to analyze empirical data in a rigorous and systematic way.

[@Carspecken1995aa]

## Reflection

*The process of turning thought back upon its own conditions of possibility.* It is the specific philosophical method used to perform Critique and to discover the Limits of Knowledge.1

The method is central to German Idealism, particularly in the work of Kant and Hegel. Kant used reflection to discover the transcendental conditions of experience.1

Reflection is the engine of Carspecken's philosophical method. He argues that it is through reflection on the act of scientific inquiry itself that we can discover the Transcendental-like conditions (e.g., the concept of substance) that science must presuppose but cannot empirically explain.1 It is a form of knowing that is not empirical-observational but is nonetheless rigorous and capable of producing valid insights.

Reflection is the methodological counterpart to Critique. It is the process by which the \"I\" discovers its own non-objectifiable nature and by which the Limits of Knowledge for any given paradigm are revealed.

[@Carspecken2016]

# S

## Self-Certainty

*Self-certainty* (*Selbstgewissheit* or *Gewissheit*) designates immediate, non-objective awareness or knowledge of oneself, constituting a pivotal concept in German Idealism [@inwood1992hegel; @caygill_kant_2004; @Carspecken:2006aa]. In Hegel, certainty characteristically appears in a subjective sense and is characterized as immediate rather than derived [@inwood1992hegel]. It represents a primitive version of self-consciousness (*Selbstbewusstsein*) [@inwood1992hegel]. The modern preoccupation with self-certainty begins with Descartes' attempt to establish the certitude of perceptions and judgment, culminating in *Cogito ergo sum* ("I think, therefore I am") [@caygill_kant_2004; @Descartes1996Meditations]. In Hegel's *Phenomenology of Spirit*, self-certainty is introduced as the initial form of self-consciousness and immediately subjected to dialectical critique. Self-certainty alone, or "self-consciousness in the narrow sense of the term," is an "abstraction" and fails to coincide with objective truth, leading to a "perpetual transcendence toward a never given adequation with itself" [@Hyppolite1974 204]. Robust self-consciousness is achievable only through reciprocal recognition---being recognized by those one recognizes [@Brandom:2019aa 49]. The certainty one has of oneself is transmitted socially: "Whomsoever I trust, his certainty of himself is for me the certainty of myself" [@Brandom:2019aa]. In the practical relation-to-self, the acting subject seeks to reassure (*vergewissern*) itself about itself as the initiator of free and attributable action through the recognition that the self's identity claims receive from others [@habermas1992individuation; @habermas_postmetaphysical_2017]. Self-certainty thus marks both an originary moment of self-awareness and an inadequate form that requires social mediation to achieve genuine self-consciousness. See also *Sense-Certainty*, *Self-Consciousness*, *Recognition*, *Apperception*.

## Self-Consciousness

*Self-consciousness* designates not empirical introspection but the fundamental logical form through which thought recognizes its own intelligible unity [@Rodl:2007aa; @Negarestani2018]. Rödl argues that judgment is inherently self-conscious: judging that things are so *is* thinking it right so to judge [@Rodl:2018aa 39]. Consciousness of validity is internal to judgment, not added through secondary reflection. The "I think" is internal to what is thought; one cannot isolate content within first-person thought that exists independently of its being thought [@Rodl:2018aa]. Self-consciousness is identical with *objectivity*: thought is objective precisely because it is self-conscious. The identity of self-consciousness and objectivity establishes the normative order governing rational activity [@Rodl:2007aa; @Negarestani2018]. Hegel emphasizes that self-consciousness achieves reality only through *recognition*: "Self-consciousness exists in and for itself when, and by the fact that, it so exists for another; that is, it exists only in being acknowledged" [@hegel1977phenomenology §178]. Self-consciousness is thus essentially social and intersubjective rather than private and introspective. The Concept (*Begriff*) is the self-consciousness of thought itself [@Rodl:2019aa]. See also *Objectivity*, *Recognition*, *Concept*, *Geist*.

## Sense-Certainty

*Sense-certainty* (*Sinnliche Gewissheit*) designates the initial stage of consciousness examined in Hegel's *Phenomenology of Spirit*, representing the foundational critique of empiricism and the "Myth of the Given" [@hegel1977phenomenology; @Brandom:2019aa]. Sense-certainty claims to be immediate knowledge, direct apprehension of objects exactly as they are, appearing as "the richest kind of knowledge" [@hegel1977phenomenology §90]. However, the dialectic demonstrates that this seemingly rich knowledge proves "the most abstract and poorest truth" [@hegel1977phenomenology §91]. When sense-certainty attempts to articulate what it knows through the 'This', 'Now', and 'Here', these terms reveal themselves as universals rather than unique particulars. The 'Now' that was night becomes stale when read at noon, yet the 'Now' itself persists as a universal structure encompassing all moments [@hegel1977phenomenology §96]. Similarly, the 'Here' remains despite changes in its specific content. Shifting focus to the 'I' produces the same result: the 'I' claiming immediate certainty proves to be a universal category [@hegel1977phenomenology §102]. Sense-certainty thus demonstrates that immediacy is impossible: all knowledge is mediated by universals. This refutes empiricism's foundational claim that sensory experience provides immediate access to particular existents. See also *Dialectic*, *Verstand*, *Vernunft*.

## Strategic Action

*Strategic action* characterizes purposive activity where success calculations include anticipation of other goal-directed actors [@Habermas1984]. Unlike *communicative action*, which coordinates through reaching understanding, strategic action achieves coordination through influencing others to serve egocentric utility calculations. This influence employs gratification, threats, suggestion, or deception rather than genuine agreement [@habermas1992individuation]. Strategic action presupposes only the objective world but enriches it to include other decision-making systems as objects of manipulation [@Habermas1984]. The rationality governing strategic action is purposive-rationality in a competitive sense, exemplified by game theory. Language in strategic action functions as a means of influence rather than as a medium of understanding. The actor treats others' responses as causal effects to be produced rather than as validity claims to be redeemed [@habermas1992individuation]. Strategic action thus systematically distorts communication when it masquerades as communicative action, creating "systematically distorted communication" where actors claim to seek understanding while actually pursuing strategic aims. This distinction is crucial for critical theory because ideology operates precisely through this confusion of strategic and communicative orientations. See also *Instrumental Action*, *Communicative Action*, *Power*.

## Sound of Time

The *Sound of Time* designates the experiential dimension of temporal awareness encountered through proprioceptive meditation practice, particularly in The Exercise. This is not literal auditory sound but the phenomenological "rhythm" or "pulse" of consciousness as it moves through arising and dissolving states. When practitioners attend carefully to bodily sensations, they encounter not static presence but dynamic temporal flow---sensations emerging, persisting briefly, and subsiding. This experiential rhythm provides the embodied ground for understanding temporality philosophically. The Sound of Time is what Hegel's dialectic of becoming articulates conceptually: the movement through which being passes into nothing and nothing into being. It is the lived experience of what Heidegger calls the "ecstatic" character of temporality---the way present experience is always already reaching toward future and retaining past. Attending to this Sound requires disciplined practice because ordinary consciousness tends to grasp experience as composed of discrete, static moments rather than recognizing the continuous temporal flow. The concept connects to Husserl's analysis of time-consciousness, particularly his account of retention and protention as structuring present experience. However, rather than approaching temporality through transcendental phenomenological analysis, the Sound of Time is encountered through sustained somatic awareness. This methodological choice embodies the commitment to praxis-grounding: philosophical insight emerging from disciplined bodily practice rather than pure conceptual analysis. See also *The Exercise*, *Becoming*, *Embodied Modal Logic*.

## Sublation (Aufhebung)

*Sublation* (*Aufhebung*) designates the dialectical process through which a concept or form is simultaneously negated (cancelled), preserved, and elevated to a new, more comprehensive level [@hegel1977phenomenology; @hegel_georg_2010]. The German *Aufhebung* captures three meanings: to cancel or annul, to preserve or maintain, and to elevate or raise up. This semantic richness is essential to Hegel's dialectical method. When a limited or one-sided determination is sublated, its inadequacy is negated, yet its truth-content is preserved and incorporated into a higher, more adequate form [@hegel1977phenomenology]. For instance, sense-certainty is sublated into perception: the claim to immediate knowledge is negated, but the insight that consciousness relates to objects is preserved and elevated within a more adequate framework that recognizes mediation. Sublation is not mere destruction but transformative negation that generates determinate results. It embodies the movement of determinate negation: each stage's collapse produces its successor by preserving what was true while overcoming what was limited. Sublation operates at every scale of dialectical development, from individual conceptual transitions to vast historical transformations. In The Exercise, sublation appears somatically: practitioners let go of awareness of one body part (negation) to feel the whole (elevation), yet the awareness of the part remains implicitly (preservation). Mathematically, the development from natural numbers to integers exemplifies sublation: subtraction's limitation (inability to subtract larger from smaller) is negated, the structure of natural number is preserved, and a richer system emerges. See also *Determinate Negation*, *Dialectic*, *Becoming*.

## Scene / Originary Scene

Scene: A purposefully chosen term, replacing more static concepts like \"context\" or \"setting.\" Drawing on Ludwig Wittgenstein, a scene is a \"scene of use\"---a dynamic, meaning-imparting context that is grasped holistically in the background of any meaningful act.1

Originary Scene: A fundamental, foundational image-cluster or root metaphor that structures an entire epistemology or philosophical tradition. It is the \"barest, broadest, and deepest meaning-imparting scene\" that underpins a way of thinking.1 Carspecken argues that much of Western thought is built on the \"originary scene of passive, solitary perception,\" which he seeks to replace with an \"originary scene of \[communicative\] action\".1

A synthesis of Wittgenstein via Henry Staten (\"scene of use\") and phenomenology (\"originary\").1

This is one of his most innovative and powerful conceptual tools, and its purposeful nature is central to his project. It allows him to perform a meta-analysis of philosophical systems by diagnosing their root metaphor. By identifying the \"originary scene\" of positivism as a lone observer viewing a stable world, he can critique its entire epistemological edifice at its foundation. By positing an alternative scene---two or more subjects attempting to coordinate action through communication---he is able to build his entire alternative epistemology based on Intersubjectivity, Praxis, and Validity Claims.

This is the foundational concept for his critique of Picture-thinking and the Metaphysics of Presence. The strategic shift in \"originary scene\" is the central move of his entire philosophical project, enabling the transition from a perception-based to an action-based theory of knowledge.

[@Carspecken1999]

## Scientism

*The ideological belief that all genuine knowledge must conform to the methods and forms of the physical sciences.* It is the combination of \"methodological naturalism\" (the idea that the methods of physical science are the only valid methods for attaining any knowledge) and \"reductionism\" (the idea that all higher-level phenomena, such as social life, can ultimately be reduced to and explained by physics) when these philosophical stances are simply assumed to be true rather than argued for.1

A long-standing term of critique in the philosophy of science and social theory used to denote an over-extension of scientific authority into domains where it is not applicable.

Scientism is the primary ideological and institutional opponent that his critical methodology is designed to combat. He describes it as a \"particularly virulent\" ideology with \"dire consequences for the social sciences,\" as it drives government and funding agencies to privilege experimental, \"what works\" research and to devalue or defund qualitative and interpretive inquiry.1 His entire philosophical argument about the Limits of Knowledge is a direct attack on the intellectual coherence of scientism.

Scientism is the political and institutional target of his philosophical Critique. His defense of the human sciences is predicated on demonstrating the philosophical untenability of scientism.

[@Carspecken2016]

## Social Site / Social Setting / Locale / Social System

*A nested set of ontological models used for research design, allowing for the analysis of the relationship between micro-level interactions and macro-level social structures.*

Social Site: A specific geographical and temporal region where routine activities and interactions take place (e.g., a particular classroom, a park).1

Social Setting: A non-physical, tacitly shared understanding between actors that defines the type of interaction they are currently engaged in (e.g., a \"joking session\" versus a \"formal lesson\" within the classroom site). Settings are fluid, co-constructed, and constantly negotiated by participants.1

Locale: The patterned social activities taking place in a geographical area surrounding a primary social site, which are linked by the routine \"time-space paths\" of actors (e.g., the school building, neighborhood, and park that surround the specific classroom).1

Social System: Broadly distributed economic, political, and cultural conditions that resource, constrain, and influence action across numerous sites and locales, often without direct face-to-face interaction (e.g., the national economy, the legal system, mass media industries). This corresponds to \"system integration\".1

These concepts are drawn from sociological theory, particularly Anthony Giddens's structuration theory, which distinguishes between \"social integration\" (face-to-face co-presence in settings) and \"system integration\" (coordination across time and space).1

These are the practical, ontological models for research design outlined in Chapter 2 of Critical Ethnography in Educational Research. They provide a concrete framework for moving from the micro-level analysis of Stage Two (Reconstructive Analysis of settings) to the macro-level analysis of Stages Four and Five (systems analysis), which seeks to explain local findings in relation to broader social structures.1

These concepts provide the concrete, empirical framework for applying his more abstract philosophical theories. Reconstructive Analysis happens within settings at a social site; systems analysis explains how those local phenomena are shaped by the broader social system.

[@Carspecken1995aa]

# V

## Validity Claims

*Validity claims* are implicit demands raised in every communicative utterance, requiring intersubjective recognition [@Habermas1984; @Carspecken1999]. Habermas identifies three primary validity claims: *truth claims* assert something about the objective world; *rightness claims* assert appropriateness relative to normative contexts; *sincerity claims* express something about the speaker's subjective world. Understanding a speech act requires grasping the conditions under which these claims would be valid [@Carspecken1999]. Validity claims are universal: they presuppose standards that transcend particular contexts and demand justification when challenged. This universality distinguishes genuine communication from merely strategic speech. In *communicative action*, coordination depends on intersubjective recognition of validity claims' force. Actors redeem claims through providing reasons that others can accept, creating the "bonding force" of reaching understanding [@habermas1992individuation]. The structure of validity claims thus institutes rationality in social practice. Carspecken emphasizes that validity claims establish normative expectations in *intersubjectivity*, creating the differentiated validity fields that structure meaning [@Carspecken1999]. Validity claims presuppose *position-taking*: to understand a claim requires adopting the speaker's position and recognizing the standards to which they appeal. See also *Communicative Action*, *Intersubjectivity*, *Position-Taking*.

## Vernunft (Reason)

*Vernunft* designates reason as the higher capacity of thought that transcends the limitations of *Verstand* (Understanding) [@hegel_georg_2010]. While initially characterized as the capacity for syllogistic inference, Vernunft is more profoundly thinking without restriction, purely self-determining, whose object is itself [@hegel_georg_2010 para. 2]. Reason is both negative and dialectical, dissolving the fixed determinations of Understanding, and simultaneously positive, generating the universal and comprehending the particular within it [@hegel_georg_2010 para. 81--82]. Vernunft operates with "dynamic metacategories" and a "holistic, developmental conception" of conceptual determinateness [@Brandom:2019aa 112--113]. Crucially, Reason "admits of the unity and identity of opposites," such as finite and infinite or subject and object [@Brandom:2019aa 50]. While Verstand conceives freedom as abstract opposite of necessity, Vernunft comprehends freedom as containing necessity as sublated within it [@hegel_georg_2010 para. 181A]. Vernunft conceives discursive norms as features of "essentially social and historical recognitive processes," developing with the attitudes that articulate them [@Brandom:2019aa]. In its truth, Reason is *Geist*---the "soul of the world" and its "innermost nature" [@hegel_georg_2010 para. 82, 439]. See also *Verstand*, *Geist*, *Dialectic*, *Concept*.

## Verstand (Understanding)

*Verstand* characterizes the capacity to form concepts, determine fixed categories, and make judgments about objects external to thought [@hegel_georg_2010 para. 80--81]. Understanding concerns itself with what is the case or with possible objects of thought, operating through judging or claiming about objects [@hegel_georg_2010 para. 2]. While Understanding allows finite things to exist with stable standing, bringing difference into selfsameness through description, it suffers inherent limitations [@hegel_georg_2010 para. 245, 80A]. Understanding's concepts (*Verstandesbegriffe*) are abstract and negative; freedom is defined merely as the abstract opposite of necessity [@hegel_georg_2010 para. 60]. Understanding operates with "static metacategories," conceiving conceptual content as having "sharp, complete boundaries" and "fixed, unchangeable inferential and incompatibility relations" [@Brandom:2019aa 112--113]. This produces "finite" truth perpetuating dualism between "merely subjective and formal activity" of thought and an objective world seen as separate and fixed [@hegel_georg_2010 para. 226, 41A]. Because it is blind to social and historical dimensions of conceptual norms, Understanding is "defective": it cannot overcome contradictions inherent in its own concepts without undermining the very contrasts it establishes [@Brandom:2019aa]. Understanding provides initial fixed determinations, but *Vernunft* (Reason) must dissolve these to reveal their internal contradictions and regenerate the universal in more concrete form. See also *Vernunft*, *Dialectic*, *Sense-Certainty*.

## Violence of Representation / Expressive Violence

Violence of Representation: The political and ethical harm that occurs when researchers from a dominant culture misrepresent, misunderstand, and misrecognize people from subordinate groups, thereby reproducing and intensifying asymmetric power relations mediated by dominant cultural categories.1

Expressive Violence: Carspecken's broader, ontological term for the unavoidable \"appropriation of the Other\" that occurs in any act of expression or representation. Because every act posits an \"identity claim\" and a \"world,\" it implicitly assigns a place to the Other, which risks objectifying them and violating their absolute, irreducible subjecthood.1

The \"violence of representation\" is a concept from post-colonial and feminist critique. \"Expressive violence\" is Carspecken's unique synthesis of this critique with the existential phenomenology of Emmanuel Levinas, who wrote of the ethical fear of \"murdering the Other\" by comprehending them.1

This conceptual pairing articulates the profound ethical challenge at the heart of ethnographic writing. The problem is not merely one of cultural bias (which can be mitigated) but a deeper ontological dilemma inherent in the act of representation itself. This dilemma cannot be \"solved,\" but it can be addressed through an ethical stance of Caritas and Ontological Forgiveness, and through forms of writing (such as poetry) that favor indirectness and create \"silences\" that respect the Other's transcendence.1

This is the fundamental ethical problem to which Caritas and Ontological Forgiveness are the proposed responses. It reveals the deep ethical stakes of his phenomenological analysis of the Subject and the necessity of Recognition.

[@HenzeCarspecken2018] The terms detailed in this glossary are not a disconnected list of concepts but the interlocking components of a complete philosophical and methodological system. The coherence of this system---its architectonics---can be traced through a clear logical progression. The project begins with a deconstructive moment: the critique of the Metaphysics of Presence and the Picture-thinking that dominates traditional epistemology. This critique reveals the untenability of any methodology based on a passive, perceptual Originary Scene.

This clearing of the ground necessitates a turn to a new foundation: the Originary Scene of communicative action and Intersubjectivity. From this new starting point, a positive theory of knowledge is built. The structure of intersubjective communication yields the universal theory of three Validity Claims, which in turn provides the systematic and rigorous toolkit for Reconstructive Analysis. This method allows the researcher to move from observable data to the deep, tacit structures of culture and power.

Finally, the methodology turns back on itself to confront its own ethical limits. The phenomenological investigation of the Subject and the act of Recognition reveals the inherent Violence of Representation. This profound ethical dilemma is then addressed through the principles of Caritas and Ontological Forgiveness. Thus, Carspecken's vocabulary charts a complete journey: from epistemological critique, to a reconstructed theory of truth, to a practical method of inquiry, and finally to a deeply considered ethical stance. It is a system of thought that successfully builds a rigorous, philosophically defensible, and ethically sensitive methodology for critical qualitative research on the very ground cleared by post-structuralist critique.

# W--Z

## The Voice

*The Voice* designates the silent, negative, and unsayable ground that must be presupposed for meaningful language to be possible [@agamben_language_2006]. Giorgio Agamben develops this concept to articulate the paradoxical foundation of linguistic meaning: language requires a ground that cannot itself be spoken yet enables all speech. The Voice is not the physical voice (the sound produced by a living being) but the absence that enables presence, the gap that makes signification possible. Agamben argues that humans are distinguished not by possessing language but by possessing Voice---the negative ground separating them from pure animality while making meaningful discourse possible [@agamben_language_2006]. This connects to ancient philosophical debates about the relationship between *phone* (voice) and *logos* (meaningful speech). The Voice represents the transition point where mere sound becomes meaningful language, yet this transition point itself cannot be captured in language. In the framework developed here, the Voice connects directly to the Null Representation ($\emptyset$): both symbolize the unrepresentable origin that enables representation. When we attend to the enabling conditions of thought through The Exercise, we encounter this silent ground---not as an object of awareness but as the very condition for awareness itself. The Voice thus functions as a philosophical symbol for what phenomenology calls the pre-reflective dimension of consciousness, what Kant called the transcendental unity of apperception, and what Hegel understood as absolute negativity. It marks the limit where representation reaches toward its own enabling conditions yet can only gesture toward them through paradoxical symbols. See also *Null Representation*, *The Exercise*, *Apperception*, *Absolute Negativity*.

## The \"I\" / Subject / \"I\"/\"me\" distinction

The \"I\"/Subject: This is not a thing, substance, or object that can be experienced or studied empirically. Rather, it is the transcendental condition for the unity and possibility of experience itself. It is the \"non-concept and non-entity\" that is presupposed in every act of knowing and acting but can never be objectified.1 It is the locus of freedom, spontaneity, and responsibility.

\"I\"/\"me\" distinction: Drawing from George Herbert Mead, Carspecken distinguishes between the \"me\" and the \"I.\" The \"me\" is the social self---the self as an object of awareness, constructed from the attitudes and perspectives of others that one has internalized. The \"I\" is the spontaneous, creative, and unpredictable response of the living organism to the social situation and the \"me.\" The \"I\" can never be captured in the present moment; as soon as one reflects on it, it has already passed and become part of the historical \"me\".1

This is a rich synthesis drawing from multiple traditions: Immanuel Kant's \"transcendental unity of apperception\" 1, Johann Gottlieb Fichte's \"positing of the I,\" G.W.F. Hegel's concept of \"the negative\" 1, Eastern non-dualist thought (e.g., Nisargadatta's \"I am not this, I am not that\") 1, and American Pragmatism (Mead's \"I\"/\"me\" distinction).1

The concept of the \"I\" as a non-objectifiable limit is central to his critique of Scientism and Picture-thinking. Because the \"I\" cannot be modeled, measured, or predicted, any science that attempts to reduce human beings to determined objects is fundamentally flawed. This \"missing infinite\" is what makes the human sciences irreducibly distinct from the physical sciences. The \"I\" is the source of human freedom and the ontological basis for his theory of Praxis.

This concept is the linchpin that connects Limits of Knowledge, Transcendental, Dialectic, The Infinite, and the experiential I-Feeling Mode. It provides the ontological foundation for his ethics of Recognition.

[@Carspecken2009aa]

# H

## Horizon (Pragmatic Horizon)

*The vast, largely tacit background of intersubjective assumptions, cultural norms, identity claims, and references to the world that makes any foregrounded, meaningful act intelligible.* Carspecken borrows the term \"horizon\" from phenomenology but relocates it from the context of perception to the pragmatic theory of meaning, focusing on communication and action.1

A pragmatic horizon is constituted by five main categories of implicit claims made with any communicative act: (1) a claim to intelligibility, (2) a claim to social legitimacy or appropriateness (Normative-evaluative), (3) a claim about the actor's subjective state (Subjective), (4) an identity claim, and (5) a reference to an Objective state of affairs.1 This horizon has both a temporal axis (the interactive syntax of past and anticipated future acts) and a paradigmatic axis (the virtual structures of contrast, similarity, and inference that give meaning to the terms and actions used).1

A synthesis of Edmund Husserl's phenomenology (the concept of a \"horizon of intelligibility\") and Jürgen Habermas's universal pragmatics (the theory of Validity Claims).1

The pragmatic horizon is a primary analytical tool in his five-stage research methodology. The work of Reconstructive Analysis is largely the process of systematically articulating the implicit structures within the pragmatic horizon of observed actions. This allows the researcher to move from the surface meaning of an utterance or action to the deep cultural norms and power structures it instantiates and reproduces.

This concept serves as the theoretical framework that contains and organizes Validity Claims, Meaning Fields, and Identity Claims. The analysis of pragmatic horizons is the core activity of Reconstructive Analysis.

[@Carspecken1995aa]

# I

## The Infinite (Missing Infinite)

*Not a reference to endlessness in space or time, but to that which is \"in-finite\" or \"not-finite\"---that which lacks the determinations, boundaries, and properties that define objects, concepts, or events.* 1 Carspecken attend to two key manifestations of the infinite: the Transcendental \"I,\" which cannot be determined as an object, and the Transcendent, which refers to postulated entities beyond direct experience.

The concept is drawn from Hegelian philosophy, via Gillian Rose's book Hegel Contra Sociology.1

The \"missing infinite\" is Carspecken's diagnosis of the core philosophical flaw in mainstream social science and even in some forms of critical theory that have been heavily influenced by post-structuralism. By operating exclusively within the confines of Picture-thinking and an Ontology of Presence, these methodologies fail to account for the \"infinite\" nature of the Subject---its un-objectifiable freedom and its status as a condition for knowledge rather than an object of knowledge. This omission leads to a reductionist view of human beings as finite, determined objects.1

This is an overarching critical concept that frames his entire critique of Picture-thinking and Scientism. It is the philosophical name for what is lost when the Subject (\"I\") is ignored, objectified, or declared \"dead.\"

[@Carspecken2018] Intersubjectivity / Intersubjective Space

Intersubjectivity: The realm of shared, co-constructed meaning, norms, and social relations that is the condition for both communication and the formation of individual selfhood. Carspecken argues that this \"subject-subject relation\" is ontologically prior to the \"subject-object relation\" that underpins empiricism.1

Intersubjective Space: A potent metaphor for the transpersonal field of possible subject positions (first-person, second-person, third-person) that is generated and structured by communicative action. It is within this \"space\" that meaning is negotiated, identities are formed, and both logical and Illocutionary inferences operate.1

The concept has roots in Hegelian phenomenology, but Carspecken's formulation is heavily indebted to American Pragmatism (George Herbert Mead) and contemporary Critical Theory (Jürgen Habermas).

Intersubjectivity serves as the foundation for his alternative, post-perceptual epistemology. He argues that even solitary thought is a form of internalized dialogue that takes place within this shared space.1 The act of understanding another person is not a mysterious psychic transference but a practical competence of \"position-taking\" within this co-constructed space.1

This is the ground upon which his theories of Communicative Action, Validity Claims, Recognition, and the \"I\"/\"me\" distinction are built. It constitutes the alternative Originary Scene that replaces the scene of solitary perception.

[@Carspecken2013aa]

# L

## Limits of Knowledge

*This term does not signify finitude or promote skepticism.* Instead, it refers to the \"enabling conditions for forms of knowledge that cannot themselves be known within the form they enable\".1 These limits are paradoxical: they are simultaneously enabling and constraining. They make a specific domain of inquiry (like physical science) possible while necessarily prohibiting that form of inquiry from studying the very conditions that ground it.1

The concept is fundamentally Kantian, derived from his project of Critique.

This is a central strategic concept in his argument against Scientism. He demonstrates that the physical sciences themselves operate within such limits. For example, the Subject (the observer/instrumental actor) and the Transcendental-like conditions (e.g., the presupposition of concepts like substance, space, and time) that make scientific inquiry possible cannot themselves be studied by scientific methods.1 This act of revealing science's own inherent limits carves out a legitimate and necessary space for other forms of inquiry---such as philosophy, hermeneutics, and the critical social sciences---which are equipped to investigate these foundational conditions.

This is the practical application of his concepts of Critique and Transcendental. It provides the core philosophical justification for his entire methodological project, which seeks to establish the unique and irreducible validity of the human sciences.

[@Carspecken2009aa]

# M

## Meaning Field

*The range of possible meanings, intentions, and interpretations that an actor, addressee, or observer might plausibly infer from a single meaningful act.* The concept acknowledges the inherent ambiguity and multi-vocality of all human communication.1

The concept is rooted in hermeneutics (the study of interpretation) and pragmatics (the study of language in use).

The meaning field is a key analytical tool in Reconstructive Analysis. The task of the researcher is not to determine the single \"correct\" meaning of an observed act, but to articulate the plausible \"meaning field\" within which the participants are operating. This is done in practice by using qualifiers like \"or,\" \"and,\" and \"and/or\" in the analysis to capture the different layers and potential ambiguities of an utterance or action.1

A Meaning Field represents the array of possibilities that are structured by the underlying Pragmatic Horizon. Articulating meaning fields is a important step in the analytical process of moving from surface phenomena to the deeper Validity Claims and cultural norms that constitute that horizon.

[@Carspecken1995aa]

# O

## Ontological Forgiveness

*A profound and fundamental form of forgiveness that addresses the unavoidable \"expressive violence\" inherent in all human action and representation.* Because every act involves an \"identity claim\" that posits a world and assigns places to others within it, it necessarily risks objectifying and misrecognizing the Other's absolute subjecthood. Ontological forgiveness is the mutual giving and receiving of pardon for this inescapable existential condition.1

This appears to be Carspecken's unique philosophical formulation, synthesizing insights from Emmanuel Levinas (the ethical demand presented by the face of the Other), Johann Gottlieb Fichte (the self-positing act as a world-positing act), and existential phenomenology.

This concept elevates the ethics of research to a profound, existential level. It suggests that even the most careful, dialogical, and critically aware research involves a degree of ontological violence simply by virtue of representing another. The ethical researcher, therefore, must be aware of this and approach their work with a stance of deep humility and an implicit need for forgiveness from those they study. He suggests that certain forms of writing, particularly poetry, are better suited to enact this forgiveness by creating \"silences\" and using indirectness to respect the Other's transcendence.1

This concept is deeply intertwined with Caritas, Recognition, and his critique of the Violence of Representation. It represents the ethical culmination of his phenomenological insights into the nature of the Subject and Intersubjectivity.

[@HenzeCarspecken2018]

# P

## Picture-thinking

*A mode of thought that uncritically relies on spatial metaphors and the model of visual perception to understand fundamental concepts like knowing, being, and consciousness.* This mode of thinking treats abstract concepts as if they were finite, bounded objects within a picture, which inevitably leads to an Ontology of Presence.1

The concept is a critique of Vorstellung (representation or picture-thinking) in Hegelian philosophy, which Carspecken explicitly acknowledges via the work of Gillian Rose.1

\"Picture-thinking\" is his primary diagnostic term for the fundamental cognitive error underlying Scientism and mainstream social science methodologies. The root metaphor is always a lone perceiver viewing an external object, a model that erases the primacy of Intersubjectivity and action.1 He argues that this mode of thought inevitably fails when it attempts to represent its own conditions of possibility (e.g., the Subject, the act of experience itself), leading to self-contradiction and the problem of the \"missing infinite.\" This is not merely a philosophical mistake but an ideological one. By framing knowledge as a neutral \"picture\" of reality, it masks the power relations and value commitments inherent in any act of knowing. This objectifying view of human beings is congruent with the needs of administrative and technical control, which lies at the core of his critique of how mainstream social science functions to reproduce oppressive social systems.1

This concept is the direct antagonist to his entire philosophical project. His concepts of The Infinite, The Subject, Praxis, and Intersubjectivity are all formulated as antidotes to the limitations of picture-thinking.

[@Carspecken2018]

## Post-secular Spirituality

*An understanding of spirituality that is grounded not in metaphysics, religion, or a belief in nonmaterial substances, but in the \"existential significance\" of the Limits of Knowledge.* It is a form of spirituality that is continuous with reason and rationality, becoming most apparent at the boundaries of what can be known, represented, or objectified.1

This is Carspecken's own formulation, designed to retrieve spiritual concerns from the domain of the irrational, where Scientism has relegated them, and re-ground them in the tradition of critical philosophy.

He connects this concept to the rich philosophical and contemplative traditions that explore the un-knowable ground of experience. This includes Kant's Transcendental \"I\", the negative dialectics of the Frankfurt School, and the non-dualistic teachings of Eastern practices like Zen Buddhism and Advaita Vedanta (as exemplified by figures like Nisargadatta Maharaj).1 It is an inquiry into the nature of the Subject and consciousness that acknowledges and respects its non-objectifiable, \"infinite\" nature.

This term serves as a bridge, linking his rigorous philosophical work on Limits of Knowledge and The \"I\"/Subject to a broader existential and contemplative tradition. It reveals the deep ethical and personal stakes of his epistemology.

[@Carspecken2009aa] Recognition

*The fundamental, intersubjective process through which a self is constituted, validated, and affirmed.* It is the experience of being \"seen\" and acknowledged by another subject as a subject, rather than as an object to be categorized or used. For Carspecken, it is a primary human need and the ontological basis of all ethics.

The concept has a rich lineage in German Idealism, particularly in Hegel's phenomenology (the master-slave dialectic). Carspecken's usage is also heavily influenced by George Herbert Mead (the self arising through taking the attitude of the other) and contemporary Critical Theory (Axel Honneth's \"struggle for recognition\").1

Recognition is the ethical core of his dialogical theory of truth. He argues that the existential \"desire for recognition\" is the deeper motivation behind what Derrida calls the \"longing for presence\".1 We seek certainty not from a neutral perception of objects, but from the affirming recognition of other subjects. Furthermore, he defines recognition as the unique mode of inference required for validating Normative-evaluative Claims: a norm is validated not by observing an external fact, but by our ability to recognize our own interests, identity, and well-being within the world that norm projects.1

This concept provides the important bridge between his epistemology and his ethics. It is the ultimate goal of Communicative Action, the basis for validating Normative-evaluative Claims, and the positive antidote to the Violence of Representation. It is the intersubjective state that Caritas and Ontological Forgiveness aim to achieve.

[@Carspecken1999]

# T

## Transcendent / Transcendental

*A important Kantian distinction that Carspecken employs to delineate the limits of empirical knowledge and critique the claims of Scientism.*

Transcendent: Refers to postulated, supersensible entities or realities that lie beyond the limits of any possible experience. This includes traditional metaphysical concepts like God, but also the unobservable entities of modern science, like quarks or strings, which are postulated to exist \"behind\" sense experience.1

Transcendental: Refers not to what is beyond experience, but to the necessary conditions for the possibility of experience itself. These conditions are not objects in experience but are the a priori structures that make experience intelligible in the first place. For Kant, these included space, time, and the categories of the understanding like \"substance\" and \"cause\".1

This distinction is central to Immanuel Kant's Critique of Pure Reason.1

This distinction is fundamental to his entire critical project. He argues that physical science itself depends on \"transcendental-like\" concepts (such as \"substance,\" \"sameness,\" \"space,\" and \"time\") that it must presuppose to function but cannot empirically investigate or prove.1 These presuppositions constitute Limits of Knowledge for science. By revealing these limits through Reflection, he demonstrates that the scientific method cannot be the sole path to knowledge, thereby carving out a legitimate and necessary space for other forms of inquiry (like philosophy and the hermeneutic sciences) that can investigate these transcendental conditions.

This is the core distinction that makes his concepts of Critique and Limits of Knowledge operational and philosophically rigorous.

[@Carspecken2016] Validity Claims (Objective, Subjective, Normative-evaluative)

*A universal feature of communicative action, whereby every meaningful utterance simultaneously raises (at least) three distinct, implicit claims to validity.* These claims can be accepted by a listener, allowing communication to continue, or challenged, requiring the speaker to provide justification.

Objective Claim: A claim about the state of the external, material world of objects and events (\"the\" world). It appeals to truth and is redeemed through evidence, observation, and the principle of multiple access.1

Subjective Claim: A claim about the speaker's internal world of intentions, feelings, and desires (\"my\" world). It appeals to truthfulness or sincerity and is redeemed through consistent behavior and honest self-expression.1

Normative-evaluative Claim: A claim about the rightness or appropriateness of an action in relation to a shared social world of norms, values, and obligations (\"our\" world). It appeals to rightness and is redeemed through reasoned argument, justification, and achieving consensus.1

The theory was developed by Jürgen Habermas in his Theory of Communicative Action.1

This is the central, practical tool for his Reconstructive Analysis. By analyzing a segment of interaction to determine which validity claims are being implicitly made, accepted, challenged, and redeemed, the researcher can systematically uncover the deep structures of meaning, power, and culture at play. It provides a non-arbitrary, rational framework for interpreting social action.

This concept operationalizes his broader theory of Intersubjectivity. The three claims correspond to the three \"ontological realms\" or \"worlds\" that are co-constituted through communication. They are the specific, analyzable elements that are reconstructed from within an act's Pragmatic Horizon.
